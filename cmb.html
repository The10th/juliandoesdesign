full app redesign Lighthouse UI In the summer of 2017 I interviewed at Lighthouse and was given a simple task: Go through the product, make note of UX issues, and design any necessary solutions.

Position
UX Interviewee
Team
Solo
When
September 2017
Time
3.5 hrs

Task

Lighthouse founding engineer, Jay, charged me with the amazingly fun task (not being sarcastic here) of exploring the Lighthouse product, designing solutions around any perceived UX issues, and BULDING THE SOLUTIONS in less than 4 hours... #letsDoThis

Initial challenges

Working with Jay (founding engineer) & Jason (founder and CEO) during my interview, I discovered, at a high-level, some common challenges Lighthouse had. Coupled with a need for some visual love, Lighthouse users needed to:

  1. be able to navigate the app quickly and easily
  2. easily access profile & app settings
  3. see relevant information and tasks at a glance

Wireframing & implementation

After exploring Lighthouse for a few minutes, and getting used to the workflow, I chose one specific page to work on: the user Dashboard. Following some light sketching and wireframing, I made some high(ish) fidelity mocks to use as a guide for the development portion of my interview. I decided to focus on a few visual tweaks:

  • lighten up the color palette a bit to resolve any distractions on the page
  • tighten up blocks of text wider than 600px for readability
  • refresh the existing components to be more *modern

Post interview

I was serendipitously given an offer on my birthday (yep, I was offered a job on my birthday), and roughly 2 weeks later my initial redesign of some core components, color palette, and app layout deployed.

Results and metrics

Over the course of 6 months Lighthouse had gone through 2 redesigns: one when I onboarded and made slight and subtle enhancements, and another 6 months later that revamped the whole UI and overall UX. Almost immediately Lighthouse received positive feedback once the overall redesign was released. Current users were delighted by the softer color palette and refreshed UI components, and the churn rate decreased from 6.45% to 2.75%!

full feature life-cycle design Manager Score

Manager Score was an observation from Lighthouse CEO, Jason Evanish, that leaders needed to be given proper insights into their performance that is candid, properly analyzed, and actionable.

In 2017, that observation became the Lighthouse manager performance companion app.

Position
UX Designer | Font-end Engineer
Team
3
When
June 2017
Time
2 months

Task

Manager Score was to be a stand alone application, able to be utilized independent of Lighthouse. Managers and leaders could utilize the tool to receive anonymous feedback from their team members - with metrics and analysis on identifying areas they could improve upon. Here, I was tasked with designing and building:

  • Marketing pages and assets (iconography, illustrations, etc...)
  • Signup and onboarding flows
  • Survey taking components
  • Data and analytics views
  • Data Exports

Assumptions

To accurately provide the best experience and UI possible for managers in need of metrics and data on their performance that is provided by their team members, we needed to make a few assumptions to ideate on.

  1. managers need to assign team members/direct reports to review them
  2. team members have to be assured that the survey they'll be taking is anonymous, increasing liklihood of candid responses
  3. surveys need to be accessed easily by team members
  4. all analyzed data need to be actionable to provide value to the manager

Challenges

Given the sensitive nature of completely candid surveys on a manager provided by their team members, there were a few challenges that were discovered during customer development research. A few FUDS (fears, uncertainties, doubts) needed to be addressed for both managers and their team members if Manager Score was to be successful.

  • managers need to trust the platform with their team memeber's emails (to provide the Manager Score survey invitation)
  • team members have to be made aware that the feedback they provide are 100% anonymous
  • managers want to know what questions are being asked on the survey

Testing & implementation

All assumptions and features need validation prior to any active commitment to a project - and that is why we utilized a wonderful tactic that helped guide the design and development of Manager Score: we beta tested with a current customer

Our findings from an initial (and very manual) beta run gave us terrific insights on how managers & their team wanted to interact with the app. We even built a feature into Manager Score from our findings from the test run.

Results

Manager Score was well received at first launch. A few iterations on the onboarding flow and metrics analysis were needed to fine tune the presentation of data, but overall successful. From a chain of fitness studios, to tech consulting firms, and Silicon Valley startups - managers, leaders, and teams of spanning a litany professions used Manager Score to gather insights on management performance.

directing leaders Skip-level Meetings Lighthouse grew with the features and enhancements the team had made through to the end of 2016. In 2017, we wanted to introduce a type of 1 on 1 meeting that helps c-level leaders (directors & executives) a way to grow & retain their employees.

Position
Product Designer | Front-end Engineer
Team
3 people
When
Q1 ~ Q2 2017
Time
4 months

Task

Building on top of the current platform's 1 on 1 meeting paradigm: managers meet with their team members : team members meet with their managers introduce a feature enabling c-level leaders to meet with team members 2 levels removed.

Initial challenges

In 2016 a fair amount of passive and active feedback requesting a "skip-levelesque" feature was received at Lighthouse. To obtain robust & detailed insights into the Skip-level needs of our users, Lighthouse dove deep into the 2016 Q4 Product Survey, and validated what had to be designed & built in Q1 2017.

  1. C-levels need to meet with employees of their reporting managers
  2. Meetings, both 1 on 1s and Skip Levels need to be very easily navigable
  3. All Meeting notes, items, and agendas from resulting Skip Level 1 on 1s need to be surfaced in regular 1 on 1s

Constraints and challenges

After metrics dissemination & initial expereince storyboarding we became aware that Skip Levels can potentially introduce disconnected and jarring expereinces for our users. While delivering a metrics proven positive feature is important and non-existent on the market, we did not want to provide users jagged experiences, possible leading to churn.

  • Provide views where c-levels could maintain current 1 on 1s with their managers while contextually providing access to their Skip Levels
  • Ensure full feature pairity with the current 1 on 1 paradigm in Skip Levels & extend the behavior of assigned meeting items
  • Provide proper controls to the c-level user, empowering them to control all types of 1 on 1s seamlessly

Results and metrics

Skip-levels launched early April of 2017 and the reception was resoundingly positive. We overcame the UX & UI hurdles for a potentially complex feature, provided our c-levels with everything they needed, and enhanced how they track meeting items between their different 1 on 1s. Current c-level users of the platform we delighted, and Lighthouse increased in company-wide monthly subscriptions expansions by 47.13% after launching Skip-levels
