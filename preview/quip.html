full saas redesign Lighthouse UI In the summer of 2017 I interviewed at Lighthouse and was given a simple task: Go through the product, make note of UX issues, and design any necessary solutions.

Project

In September 2017 Lighthouse was interviewing for a designer & engineer to work on the visuals of the application & develop the front-end. The core Lighthouse application, at that time, had not received much visual design love while organically growing more robust feature-sets to fulfill the needs of the users on the platform. Onboarding a new designer & engineer meant that the indivudal undertaking the task needed to understand the users as well as the mission of the company. As the interviewee for the role, I performed high-level discovery, wireframing, and UX/UI redesign of Lighthouse's main workflows & userbase. Apart from myself, the team consisted of the founding engineer and the CEO. 

Lighthouse's pre-redesign UI in 2016

Design & Implementation

Working with the team at Lighthouse uncovered a few enlightening bits of information that would influence my design. I was able to understand who the target users were, discovered a few hiccups in experience that were reported by the users, and uncovered some potential issues that would impact the app's ability to scale. Having a firm set of challenges to work with, I set some objectives for myself that I could achieve in a short amount of time:

Design Objectives
  • Give the UI components a more moden touch
  • Surface common elements that users reported being hard to find
  • Clean up font declarations to resolve hierarchy issues

To achieve maximum visual impact in a small amount of time I decided to lightly wireframe some components & layouts, and prototype mostly in browser.

Visual Changes

The key, and arguably the most visually impactful, component of the redesign was to update and modernize the overall visuals of the app. Lighthouse already had a few visual elements in place, including a logo, color palette, and some UI components, though they didn't not have a firm styleguide, which enabled me to work in some creativity.

Color Palette

The overall Lighthouse color palette to me was bold and striking; while the colors were captivating, I felt them to be a bit distracting at time while navigating a text-editing & reading heavy application. I decided to lighten the greens and minimize total amount of greys that were being used.

Typography

Similar to the color palette, Lighthouse's main typeface was very striking and did not lend well to a text heavy application. I was inclined to change the typeface to one that was widely available, easy to read, provides a wide-ish range of fonts, and has a it of character. In the end I opted for Open Sans, as it fulfilled my requisites and is widely used on the web - giving Lighthouse an air of familiarity.

Layout

To free up some vertical real estate, I decided to change the layout of the core app navigation; this would also serve to preserve information hierarchy and help the app scale in the future.

Results & Reception

In the end, some quick sketches, wireframes, and in-browser prototyping from a UX interview evolved into an entire redesign for Lighthouse. The redesign was very well received by users, and resulted in a decrease in churn, going from 6.45% to 2.75%!

manager performance app Manager Score

An app that allows leaders to be given proper insights and analytics into their performance, by their team members.

Insights are candid, properly analyzed, and most importantly actionable.

Position
UX Designer | Font-end Engineer
Team
3 people
When
June 2017
Time
2 months

Task

Work with the Lighthouse team to design, build, and iterate a companion application to Lighthouse that helps managers gain insight on their performance.

  • Marketing pages and assets (iconography, illustrations, etc...)
  • Signup and onboarding flows
  • Survey UI components
  • Data and analytics views
  • Data Exports

Assumption

Managers need actionable insights from their team and team members need to answers survey questions about their manager's performance. Given this assumption, a basic workflow was ideated on:

  1. Managers need to request feedback from their team members
  2. Team members have to be confident that survey answers will remain anonymous
  3. Surveys need to be accessed easily by team members

Challenges

Completely candid surveys on a manager's performance posed a few challenges that were uncovered during customer development research. Some common FUDS that surfaced were:

  • Managers need to trust the platform with their team member's emails
  • Team members have to be informed that the feedback they provide are 100% anonymous
  • Managers want to know what questions are being asked on the survey

User Testing

To validate our designs and assumptions, prior to active project development, we enlisted the help of one of Lighthouse's long-time users to alpha test the product.

Findings from initial (and very manual) tests provided terrific insights on how managers & their teams wanted to interact with the product. Unplanned features were even built, resulting directly from UX research.

Results

Manager Score was well received and kept gaining momentum. From a chain of fitness studios, to tech consulting firms, and Silicon Valley startups - managers, leaders, and teams spanning many professions use Manager Score to gather insights on management performance. Some have even run quarterly scores to track improvement!

complex feature implementation Skip-level Meetings Building on top of Lighthouse's 1 on 1 meeting platform: managers meet with their team members : team members meet with their managers, introduce a feature enabling executives, directors, VPs, and managers of managers to meet with team members.

Position
Product Designer | Front-end Engineer
Team
3 people
When
Q1 ~ Q2 2017
Time
4 months

Initial Discovery

We dove deep into Lighthouse's 2016 Q4 Product Survey in order to understand what had to be designed & built for Q1 2017, and validate base assumptions of how skip-level meetings would work:

  1. Users need to meet with employees of their reporting managers regularly
  2. Skip-level 1 on 1s need to have the same meeting navigation UI
  3. All meeting notes, action items, and notes from skip-level meetings need to be surfaced in regular 1 on 1s

Challenges

Reading survey answers and running UX interviews with current Lighthouse customers surfaced quite a few insights and possible challenges in the design process:

  • Managers of managers have varying frequencies of 1 on 1 meetings - most are not consistent
  • Privacy & information integrity is exceptionally important; users need absolute confidence in the feature
  • Users want to control meeting items & frequencies without breaking the context of their 1 on 1s

Results and Metrics

After addressing some key concerns that Lighthouse's users had about using Skip-levels, the feature launched early April of 2017 and the reception was resoundingly positive. Overcoming hurdles by:

  • Providing language and iconography that communicates to the user that meeting notes are fully secure and private
  • Designing the feature in a way that contextually preserves the content of both regular and skip-level meetings
  • Actively listening, testing, and iterating on solutions with the users and their workflow

Directors, vps, and managers of managers using the platform were delighted, and Lighthouse increased in company-wide monthly expansions by 47.13%!
